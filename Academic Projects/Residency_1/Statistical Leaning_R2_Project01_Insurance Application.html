Questions to be answered¶

a. Do charges of people who smoke differ significantly from the people who don't?¶

In [59]:
charges_smoker = main_df[main_df['smoker'] == 'yes']['charges'] #only charges of those who smoke
charges_nonsmoker = main_df[main_df['smoker'] == 'no']['charges'] # only charges of those who don't smoke

H_Null: Charges of customers who smoke = Charges of customers who don't smoke

H_Alternative: Charges of customers who smoke != Charges of customers who don't smoke

In [63]:
sns.distplot(charges_smoker)
sns.distplot(charges_nonsmoker);
In [60]:
stats.ttest_ind(charges_smoker, charges_nonsmoker)
Out[60]:
Ttest_indResult(statistic=46.664921172723716, pvalue=8.271435842177219e-283)

Observation: Since the p-value is so small (e-283), we reject null hypothesis. Hence the charges are different. Also visible from the distribution plot above, there is a very significant difference between the charges for smokers and non-smokers¶

b. Does bmi of males differ significantly from that of females?¶

In [65]:
bmi_male = main_df[main_df['sex'] == 'male']['bmi']
bmi_female = main_df[main_df['sex'] == 'female']['bmi']

H_Null: bmi of males = bmi of females

H_Alternative: bmi of males != bmi of females

In [66]:
sns.distplot(bmi_male)
sns.distplot(bmi_female);
In [108]:
stats.ttest_ind(bmi_male, bmi_female)
Out[108]:
Ttest_indResult(statistic=1.696752635752224, pvalue=0.08997637178984932)

Observation: Since the p-value is higher than .05, we fail to reject the null Hypothesis. i.e There are no differences between the bmi of males and females. Also there is no significant difference between the bmi of males and females visible from the graph.¶

d. Is the distribution of bmi across women with no children, one child and two children, the same ?¶

In [84]:
bmi_nochild = main_df[(main_df['sex'] == 'female')&(main_df['children'] == 0)]['bmi'] #female with no child
